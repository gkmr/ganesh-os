# ADR-17 - Store-of-record cutover, the two-lane end state, and the GV-class reader

**Status:** Accepted · 2026-07-13 · extends [ADR-14](adr-14-one-store-triple-channel.md) (the one-store law) and the ADR-15 two-engine law ([decisions.md](decisions.md)) · applies the [ADR-13](adr-13-channel-strategy.md) pause and re-entry ladder.

BLUF: the one store moved. The task corpus migrated from the on-device reminders store to a cloud-API task store in one manifested, reversible pass; the old engine stays readable behind a small carve-out and a verdict-gated retirement check-in. The end state is two lanes with one job each: the cloud lane discovers, triages, and asks; the local lane adapts channels and holds the carve-outs. A risky third-party channel re-entered as a permanent read-only reader, and the recipient swap recorded the safety architecture working: agents refused an in-band redirect of personal messages until the operator confirmed first-party.

## Context

Four pressures converged on one day:

1. ADR-14 made the on-device reminders store the ONLY store of actionable items, but the discovery and triage lane had since moved to a hosted platform that cannot reach on-device stores. Every hosted task was either blind or working from mirrors - and a mirror of the one store is exactly the state leakage the one-store law exists to prevent.
2. The two-engine law (ADR-15) had been proven for an engine swap over a shared store, but never for a store swap, where source and destination have different data models: priorities, tags, native recurrences, and checklists all needed explicit mapping rather than a copy.
3. The third-party channel fully paused under the ADR-13 ladder reached its verdict day, and the verdict had to answer a harder question than "is it safe to come back" - namely, which half of the channel was ever the risk.
4. The operator's canonical text-channel recipient had to change, and the change request arrived in-band, through the same message lane the agents parse for instructions.

## Options

- Keep the on-device store and build a sync bridge for the hosted lane - a permanent two-store system; ADR-14 exists because state leaking into views was the disease.
- Big-bang migrate and delete the old store the same day - no rollback line, and a direct violation of the two-engine law.
- **Migrate the corpus to the cloud-API store in one manifested pass, hold the old engine read-only behind a carve-out and a gated retirement check-in, and land the two-lane end state with the fences unchanged.**

## Decision

1. **Store-of-record cutover, manifested.** The full corpus (roughly 200 items across 13 projects) moved in one pass to a consumer task platform with a real cloud API: priorities mapped exactly onto the destination's scale, tags carried, recurrences recreated natively instead of simulated, and checklists preserved with their done-states. Every migrated item carries a backref id to its source record, and the pass wrote a migration manifest (source id to destination id, row by row), so the move is auditable and reversible per item, not per catastrophe. Ambiguous rows (33 of them) went to a REVIEW queue for the operator instead of being guessed. The source store was not modified by the migration.
2. **Two engines coexist read-only until a verified round-trip; retirement is a human verdict.** From cutover minute one the new store is the only write path; the old engine stays alive and readable. Its remaining duties are one carve-out lane (health items whose hardware integrations are on-device) and evidence: a scheduled one-shot check-in three days out asks the operator RETIRE / NOT YET / KEEP MIRROR, by which point the full loop - create, render, reply, apply, verify - will have round-tripped on the new store for three days. The old engine retires on that verdict, never on a timeout.
3. **The two-lane end state.** The hosted lane owns discovery (vendor diffs plus the primary broad scan), triage (priority and tier writes through the store's API), and the ask loop (morning plan, evening wrap, an operator command lane). The local lane owns what the cloud cannot reach: the consumer-messaging reply loop, on-device inboxes, the carve-out lanes, and the monitor stack that watches both lanes. The store being reachable from both lanes means lane placement is now a capability and scheduling decision, not a data-access one - and the single-writer fence survived the split untouched: every mutable field kept exactly one owning agent, and the fence eval did not change.
4. **The GV-class reader: a risky channel re-enters read-only, permanently.** The paused channel came back as a reader and nothing else: digest reads twice a day, zero writes, forever, with a health monitor alerting on bridge death. This names a channel class rather than a probation step: when a channel's platform risk attaches to automated sending (the thing that triggered the suspension), reading is the survivable posture, and read-only is the classification, not a waypoint on the road back to writing. The class already had one quiet member - a voice-number inbox read through mail - so the re-entry made it a pattern with a name instead of a coincidence.
5. **The recipient swap and the injection pause.** The operator's canonical recipient changed, and the request arrived in-band. Multiple agents independently refused to redirect personal-message delivery on in-band say-so and paused for first-party confirmation through the operator's own channel before executing the fleet-wide swap - doctrine override first, then every prompt and hook, then a delivered test message to the new thread. This is recorded as an anecdote deliberately: an instruction that changes where personal data flows is treated as a potential injection until the principal confirms out-of-band, and the refusal was the designed behavior, not friction to engineer away.

## Consequences

The hosted lane went from blind to first-class, and the one-store law got stronger, not weaker, by the store moving: files, boards, and digests remain renderings, and now both lanes render from the same substrate. The migration pattern - manifest, backrefs, a REVIEW queue for ambiguity, source untouched, retirement gated on a human verdict after a verified round-trip - turns a store swap into the same reversible experiment ADR-15 made of an engine swap. The costs, named: a consumer cloud API is now load-bearing (mitigated by the manifest and backrefs, which keep a full rebuild path, and by the old engine staying readable until retirement); the carve-out is a deliberate second store surface, held to one lane and one domain; the REVIEW queue adds one human pass per migration; and the GV-class forgoes a send channel permanently - accepted, because the suspension already demonstrated which direction of traffic gets the channel killed. The injection pause costs one confirmation round-trip on any in-band request to redirect personal data, and that latency is the point.
